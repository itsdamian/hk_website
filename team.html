團隊總監

Team Director

楊少詩博士是心理學系的副教授。她一直在教育心理學、發展心理學、認知心理學和有關有特殊需要的幼兒等領域任教。她是我們的團隊負責人，她主要負責有關與第二語言學習、閱讀介入、實證為本、學生學習和雙語能力發展相關的研究項目。她的論文是關於以英語為第二語言的幼兒的語音意識與閱讀之間的關係。她的研究圍繞幼兒的語言和第二語言的閱讀發展。楊博士其他研究重點包括與語言和閱讀發展相關的情感和認知的因素以及閱讀介入的效果。目前，她的研究調查兒童的正常和非典型英語閱讀發展、認知語言基礎和第二語言學習的情感過程以及家庭環境在語言學習中的作用。她還研究瞭解如何在各種技術平台上優化學習。

Dr. Yang Siu-sze is an Associate Professor in the Department of Psychology. She has been teaching in the areas of educational psychology, developmental psychology, cognitive psychology, and young children with special needs. She is our team leader and is primarily responsible for research projects related to second language learning, reading intervention, evidence-based practices, student learning, and bilingual development. Her research examines the effects of morphological awareness, orthographic awareness, and phonological awareness in Chinese (L1) and English (L2) word reading and reading comprehension in Chinese children with longitudinal and intervention approaches. These metalinguistic skills have been found to be crucial in developing reading skills in Chinese learners. Currently, her research investigates children's typical and atypical English reading development, cognitive-linguistic foundations and affective processes of second language learning, and the role of home environment in language learning. She also studies how to optimize learning on various technology platforms.

TONG's research primarily investigates the causal relationship between syntactic awareness—the ability to understand sentence structure—and reading comprehension, including difficulties in reading comprehension among bilingual children. She employs both longitudinal designs and intervention approaches to examine this relationship. Tong has identified syntactic awareness as a key factor influencing reading comprehension development in bilingual children, both with and without reading comprehension difficulties[85, 86]. Recently, TONG and colleagues proposed a theoretical model illustrating how syntactic skills impact reading comprehension through two core pathways. The first, the syntax-to-lexicon pathway, suggests that syntactic skills influence the construction of lexical representations, ultimately affecting comprehension. The second, the syntax-to-sentence pathway, indicates that these skills shape how readers parse sentences and predict upcoming information. This model offers testable hypotheses designed to enhance effective instructional practices for developing bilingual children's reading comprehension in both their L1 and L2.
